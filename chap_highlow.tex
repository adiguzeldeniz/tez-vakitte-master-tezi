\chapter{Non Linear Excitation limit}
\section{Motivation}
M\"ossbauer nuclei have a very narrow spectral linewidth. This feature renders M\"ossbauer nuclei as ideal candidates for high-precision hyperfine spectroscopy. On the other hand, this also means, that a very small fraction of the delivered photons from the accelerator based pulsed x-ray sources are the resonant photons. That is  why experiments involving M\"ossbauer nuclei have been limited to low excitation regime (LER).\\

However with the new x-ray free electron lasers (XFELs), a significant improvement of the number of nuclear-resonant photons per pulse can be achieved. This raises the question about how to model the nuclear dynamics in the non-linear regime. In LER, one approximates $\rho_{gg} = 1 $, so the equations (\ref{rhoee}-\ref{rhoeg}) and (\ref{waveeq}) can be decoupled to obtain an analytical solution by fourier transforming and then solving the time dependent differential equation (\Cref{section_respfunction}). This is in non linear excitations not the case. It is not possible to obtain an analytical Equation of Motions in higher excitations, because in that case the equations do not decouple and one has to solve 5 coupled partial differential equations.\\

On the other hand, numerical methods can be used to simulate these equations. We modeled the Maxwell-Bloch equations in Julia programming language by using the method of lines (MOL). In this chapter we will first discuss the robustness and limitations of our model and compare our solutions with the analytical response function in the low excitation regime.\\

Wolff et.al. \cite{wolff2023characterization} explored the low excitation regime (LER) and slightly beyond, proposing signatures to characterize the LER. They calculated perturbatively that the ratio between coherent and incoherent light remains constant over time in the LER, but not beyond. We will discuss their results and compare them with ours. Notably, their study did not consider propagation effects. Our model, however, incorporates these effects. After comparing our results against \cite{wolff2023characterization} in the non-propagating or thin-target limit, we will extend our discussion to the nuclear dynamics and time response of thick targets.

Due to the discretization of the spatial component, the target is divided into N coupled differential equations, which in \cref{benchmark_plot} is $N=400$ (\cref{table:the_parameters}). We refer to the solution of each differential equation as a slice. Consequently, our target is split into $N$ slices.
With our model, we can plot every slice, allowing us to observe how light propagates through different thicknesses of the target. Here, every 50th slice is plotted starting from the 5th. When examining the slices, we expect the Bessel beats to shift. This can be explained as follows: the parameter b which determines the exact positions of the Bessel beats in \cref{responsefunction}, can be calculated as $b = z_\mathrm{max} \cdot \eta /2$. Therefore, when plotting a specific slice, our effective $z_\mathrm{max}$ changes, because it depends on N. As a result the $b$ should be calculated as $b = z_\mathrm{max}\cdot  (N_\mathrm{plot} / N) \cdot \eta /2$. \\

In \cref{benchmark_plot}, the top panel shows $|\Omega|^2$, the actual observable in an experiment. The Bessel beats predicted by the analytical calculation in the LER can be seen. In the very thin target limit, represented by slice 5 in the plot, no Bessel beats are observed. This is due to the non-propagating limit: if there are not enough scatterers, no multiple scattering emerges, resulting in only exponential decay and the absence of Bessel dynamics.\\



That is  how we can see that for a thicker target the Bessel beats arise in a shorter time span compared to a thinner target for which the Bessel beats arise in a longer time span. \\

In the bottom three plots in \Cref{benchmark_plot}, the nuclear dynamics of the two level system is plotted. Before the light pulse comes, all nuclei are in ground state, so we would expect $\rho_{gg} = 1$ and $\rho_{ee} = 0$, both of which can be seen on the plots. After the initial pulse, some of the atoms get excited. So there is a drop in intensity of $\rho_{gg}$ and a peak in $\rho_{ee}$. These excited atoms would then decay to ground state, which can also be seen after some time. \\

Lastly, in the bottom right part in \cref{benchmark_plot}, $|\rho_{eg}|^2$ is plotted. Here we can see the phenomena that was introduced in the background chapter, that the dynamics of the dipole moments arise in a shorter time span compared to the $\Omega$. This is evident when comparing the two quantities. For example for slice 355, the first dip occurs $0.8\gamma^{-1}$ in $|\rho_{eg}|^2$, whereas in $\Omega$ it first appears around $2\gamma^{-1}$. We will explore the comparison between these two quantities in more depth below.\\

Another important property of the plot is that due to the rather low speed of light ($u$) that was used to solve EOM's, we observe a noticeable time shift between the first and the last slice. This can be seen in \cref{fig:ananum_compare}, where the light pulse of slice 5 starts at around $0 \; \gamma t$ but the pulse from slice 355 comes at $ 0.2-0.3 \gamma^{-1}$ later. This effect can be calculated as $t_\mathrm{shift} = u \cdot z_\mathrm{max}\cdot  (N_\mathrm{plot} / N) $.\\

In \cref{fig:ananum_compare}, a very good agreement can be seen between the theoretical and the numerical solution in the low excitation regime. Here, the last slice is plotted and to compensate the time shift caused by the speed of light, the theoretical solution was shifted by $u \cdot z_\mathrm{max}$. In figure \ref{fig:rhoegananum_compare} the last slice of $|\rho_{eg}|^2$ is plotted with the basis set of values and a pulse area of $\mathcal{A} = 0.01\pi $. Here we also achieve a very good agreement between the analytically calculated $\rho_{eg}$ in time domain and the numerically calculated solution. \\

To summarize shortly, we can model the nuclear dynamics and light propagation in the target for different observables and we find good agreements between the numerical solutions calculated by our model and the theoretical solutions.

\subsection{Propagation effects in LER} \label{section:propeffectsLER}
Before we discuss propagation effects, we need to introduce further observables. We distinguish between coherently and incoherently scattered light. In incoherent scattering, also called the $4\pi$ scattering, the nuclei decay independently, so the observed signal intensity is proportional to the sum of the population of the excited states $\rho_{ee}$ \cite{Agarwal1974, qunatuminterference}. The coherently scattered light is highly directional and therefore can be observed as the propagated light, which is $|\Omega|^2$. Since \cite{wolff2023characterization} did not take into account the propagation effects, the coherent light can also be calculated as $|\rho_{eg}|^2$ in the non-propagating limit, which is proportional to induced dipole moments. For our discussion, we will use the term incoherent light $I_\mathrm{inc}$, when referring to the summed $\rho_{ee}$ over all slices. This is because, in an experimental setting, it is not feasible to measure the incoherently scattered light from every slice in a setting like this, where we have $400$ slices; instead, the overall summed $\rho_{ee}$ would be measured. \\


Now we can continue and look at the ratio of $|\rho_{eg}|^2 / \rho_{ee}$ in the low excitation regime as well as the propagation limit of thicker targets . In \Cref{fig:lowexc_ratio} this ratio is plotted for 3 different slices: 5, 200 and 400. Because we are calculating with a total number of slices $N = 400$ (\cref{table:the_parameters}), plots can also be seen from going left to right as going from an extremely thin target to a thick target. \\

    \centering

    \caption{The ratio $|\rho_{eg}|^2 / \rho_{ee}$ is plotted for different excitations, ranging from pulse area $\mathcal{A} = 0 \pi$ to $0.012 \;\pi$. The slices 5, 200, 400 are plotted out of the total number of slices $N =  400$. The colorbar shows the intensity in arbitrary units.  } 
    \label{fig:lowexc_ratio}
\end{figure}

On the very left panel in \Cref{fig:lowexc_ratio}, in the non-propagating limit, we recover the same results as \cite{wolff2023characterization}, namely that the ratio $|\rho_{eg}|^2/\rho_{ee}$ is constant over time. In the middle and the very right panel in \cref{fig:lowexc_ratio}, we can go to the thicker targets and analyse. In this case this ratio $|\rho_{eg}|^2/\rho_{ee}$ changes, to generalize the results from \cite{wolff2023characterization} in the propagation limit. Here, we already see some propagation effects, as dips in the figure. Only in the \textit{ultra-low excitation regime} ($<\mathcal{A} = 0.001\,\pi$), the ratio $|\rho_{eg}|^2/\rho_{ee}$ stays constant. The mechanism that results in these dips is the modified dynamics of $\rho_{ee}$ in the LER, which we will go in more depth in the following sections and in \Cref{fig:4Block_ratio_cohinc}. \\

As a next step, we can look at the ratio of $|\Omega|^2/\rho_{ee}$, which can be seen in \Cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc}. Similar to \Cref{fig:lowexc_ratio}, we can think of the plots as going from a very thin target to a thick target from left to right. On the thin target limit (left), we can see that the ratio is constant, agreeing with \cite{wolff2023characterization}. This is due to the fact, that in the thin target limit, we have no propagation effects, thus no coherent scattering dynamics. As a result, the ratio $|\Omega|^2/\rho_{ee}$ is constant over time. \\

We can also observe this ratio in thicker targets, as shown in the middle and the right panels of \Cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc}. Here, the ratio $|\Omega|^2/\rho_{ee}$ deviates significantly from the non-propagating limit. In thicker targets, this ratio is no longer constant over time, and exhibits propagation effects in the form of Bessel beats. In the middle and right panels of \cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc}, we see bright yellow lines representing peaks and dark purple lines indicating dips. To better understand these structures, we can plot individual slices from \Cref{fig:lowexc_ratio} and \Cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc}. \\

In \Cref{fig:4Block_ratio_cohinc}, the ratios $|\rho_{eg}|^2/\rho_{ee}$ and $|\Omega|^2/\rho_{ee}$ are plotted for an individual solution with a pulse area $\mathcal{A} = 0.0011 \pi$, in the non-propagating and in the thick target limit. This can also be seen as taking a horizontal slices from \cref{fig:lowexc_ratio} and \cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc} corresponding to the pulse area of $\mathcal{A} = 0.0011 \pi$. In \Cref{fig:4Block_ratio_cohinc}, on the top row, the ratio $R(t) = |\rho_{eg}|^2/\rho_{ee}$ is plotted for a thin target on the left side and for a thick target on the right side, as we plotted in \Cref{fig:lowexc_ratio}. The same is done for the second row: the ratio $|\Omega|^2 / \rho_{ee}$ is plotted for a thin target (left) and for a thick target (right), also same as in \Cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc}. \\

Looking at the top left plot in \cref{fig:4Block_ratio_cohinc}, there are no propagation effects, so both $\rho_{eg}$ and $\rho_{ee}$ show an exponential decay. As a result the ratio between the two is constant, which coincides with the result from \cite{wolff2023characterization}. Going to the thick target limit, as can be seen on the right top panel in \cref{fig:4Block_ratio_cohinc}, $\rho_{ee}$ shows some different dynamics as compared to $|\rho_{eg}|^2$. We will also see this effect in beyond LER, where it will be clearer to observe. This effect can be explained by looking at the equation for $\rho_{ee}$ in \cref{rhoee_act}. It consists of two parts; the first part is the exponential decay part and the second part is the coupling to $\rho_{eg}$. In the low excitations, the population of the excited states $\rho_{ee}$ is very small due to the pulse area theorem (\cref{section:pulseareatheorem}). As a result, we see the Bessel dynamics of $\rho_{eg}$ also in the $\rho_{ee}$ in low excitations. Going beyond low excitations, $\rho_{ee}$ gets bigger in \cref{rhoee_act} and the exponential decay starts to dominate the equation. This then results in a \textit{washing out} of the Bessel beats, as can be seen in top right panel in \Cref{fig:4Block_ratio_cohinc}. As we are plotting the ratio between $|\rho_{eg}|^2$ and $\rho_{ee}$, exactly these deviations are the main mechanism that results in the dips, which can be seen in \Cref{fig:lowexc_ratio}. \\




Looking at the bottom left plot in \cref{fig:4Block_ratio_cohinc}, there is no propagation effects again, as we are in the thin target limit, so only exponential decay can be observed. Going over to the thick target limit, the yellow and orange regions in \cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc} become clearer. Calculating the ratio, we are dividing $|\Omega|^2$ to $\rho_{ee}$. Because the $\rho_{ee}$ is coupled to $\rho_{eg}$ as explained above, we do observe the dynamical patterns that we see in $\rho_{eg}$, also in the $\rho_{ee}$. As a result when calculating the ratio, we get a dip, if $\Omega$ has a dip, and we get a peak, if $\rho_{ee}$ has a dip. \\

So the yellow lines can be understood as the dips, that result from the $J_0$ Bessel beats from $\rho_{eg}$, which can also be seen in $\rho_{ee}$ in LER as a result of the coupling between the two. The dark purple lines are the dips from the $\Omega$, which is governed by the $J_1$ Bessel function. So the different lines that we see in \Cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc}, correspond to different zero crossings of the $J_0$ and $J_1$ Bessel functions. \\

To summarize our findings until now, we were able to recover the results from \cite{wolff2023characterization} in the low excitation regime in the non propagating limit. We also observed the ratio $|\rho_{eg}|^2/\rho_{ee}$ in thick targets, where we see some deviations from the non-propagating limit. We observed modified dynamics in $\rho_{ee}$, which is dependent from the excitation. This dynamics then manifests itself in the ratio $|\rho_{eg}|^2/\rho_{ee}$ in thick targets. As a result, the ratio is not constant in the propagation limit. \\

Other than that, also the ratio $|\Omega|^2/\rho_{ee}$ is analysed for thin and thick targets. Here we also see these modified dynamics of $\rho_{ee}$ and propagation effects in $\Omega$, as well as in $\rho_{ee}$, that results in peaks and dips, when the ratio $|\Omega|^2/\rho_{ee}$ is calculated.

\subsection{Summed incoherent radiation as an observable}
Even though we can pick out single slices from our target and have a discussion about how $\Omega$, $\rho_{eg}$ and $\rho_{ee}$ and their ratios evolve over time including the propagation effects, these observables are experimentally not easily accessible. The incoherent light is more easily measured as a function of time, which is then summed over all the emitted incoherent light from \textit{all} the slices, and not just one. So in this section we will go more in depth about the summed incoherent radiation and the ratio between the coherent light. In the following, we will be denoting the summed incoherent light over the target as $I_\mathrm{inc}$. To calculate the summed emitted incoherent radiation, we use $  I_\mathrm{inc} =\sum_{i=1}^{N} \rho_{ee,i}$.\\

Due to the averaging, the structures which were a result of the coupling between $\rho_{ee}$ and $\rho_{eg}$, can not be observed anymore. Instead the incoherent light has some slight oscillations and a peak, which can be identified after the arrival of the incident light pulse at approximately $0.5 \, \gamma t$. To understand these structures, we can observe the $\rho_{ee}$ for three different distinct pulse areas, as ultra-low , middle-low and low excitations. \\

Therefore, $\rho_{ee}$ is plotted in a 2D plot as a function of slices and time in \Cref{fig:threeblock_rhoee}. Here we see two remarkable things: first, the Bessel beats, due to the coupling to $\rho_{eg} $, can be seen in the target, which shift depending on the slices. This is clear, as the argument of the Bessel function of $\rho_{eg}$ in \Cref{rhoegana} is $2\sqrt{b\tau}$. Due to this relation, a quadratic form is expected by Bessel beats with increasing thickness, which is the case, going from the first slice to the last.\\

Now we can interpret the peaks in \Cref{fig:summed_rhoee}. The averaging of $\rho_{ee} $ can basically be seen as summing up the 2D plots in \cref{fig:threeblock_rhoee} along y-axis. Here Bessel beats always shift to the earlier times when considering the thickness but the shift slows down due to the quadratic behaviour. There is an area in the early times, where there is no Bessel beats to be seen, which then do give rise to the peaks that we see in \Cref{fig:summed_rhoee} when summed over all the slices. Other than that, the slight oscillations on the curves in \Cref{fig:summed_rhoee} can be interpreted as the interference of Bessel beats, corresponding to multiple targets with different thicknesses. \\

As a last important point in the LER, we can observe the coherent to incoherent light, as discussed in \Cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc}, but now with the summed incoherent radiation. For this we plotted $I_\mathrm{coh}/I_\mathrm{inc}$ in \Cref{fig:Actual_cohinc_ratio}, where $I_\mathrm{coh}$ is given by $|\Omega|^2$ and $I_\mathrm{inc} = \sum_i \rho_{ee,i}$. Here we can see that the ratio of coh/inc stays constant as a function of pulse area in the low excitation regime, which is expected in the LER. The Bessel beats from the coherent light dominates the spectrum, that we see as a time response. Comparing \cref{fig:Actual_cohinc_ratio} to the \cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc}, we can no longer see the peaks from the latter figure in the first figure. This is due to the summing up of $\rho_{ee}$, which we discussed in \Cref{fig:summed_rhoee}. \\

To summarize our findings, the summed incoherent radiation is calculated as the sum of $\rho_{ee}$ over all the slices, which is the experimentally more easily accessible observable, instead of the incoherent radiation emitted from a single slice. Here we saw interesting structures like a peak and slight oscillations in the incoherent light intensity plotted in \Cref{fig:summed_rhoee}. This was then explained by analysing how $\rho_{ee}$ evolves in the target as a function of time-slices plot for different pulse areas. We were able to explain, that the peak arises due to the lack of Bessel beats in the early times and the slight oscillations are due to constructive/destructive interference of different Bessel beats corresponding to different thickness in the target. As a last point, we also analysed the ratio of coherent to incoherent light intensities. Here only the Bessel dynamics of $|\Omega|^2$ could be observed, as the Bessel dynamics of $\rho_{ee}$ get lost in the summation process.\\

\section{Beyond the LER}\label{section:beyondLER}
Until now we looked at the dynamics of nuclear two level systems in the low excitation regime. With our model, we can also go on to the higher excitations, to the population inversion and beyond. For this, we will start off by showing the main result of this thesis in \Cref{fig:Omega_highexc}. Here similar to \cref{fig:lowexc_omegarhoeg}, the $|\Omega|^2$ is plotted as a function of excitation and time. Contrary to low excitation regime, we are seeing substantial deviations from the linear response function formalism. The Bessel beats stay in the same shape, but they become shifted in time as the excitation increases. Around the population inversion these beats get shifted so much, that they connect to the second beats perfectly, which does give rise to \textit{avoided crossing like} structures between the first and second Bessel beats. From now on, we will be calling these structures as \textit{transitions}. In the next figures we will be trying to understand this phenomena by dissecting the $\Omega$ in different plots, such as time/slice plot or excitation/slice plots and also looking at the other observables such as $\rho_{ee}$, the incoherent radiation as an averaged $\rho_{ee}$ and also the $\rho_{eg}$. 



\subsection{$\Omega$ under the microscope} \label{section_omegamicroscope}
First, we want to start off by examining the transition in more depth. For that a zoom around the population inversions is shown in \Cref{fig:highres_zoom2block}. Here the transition do not take place abruptly, but it is rather a gradual shift around the population inversions. Also an interesting feature is that the second transition is at $\mathcal{A} = 2 \pi$, whereas the first crossing is at $\mathcal{A} = 1.024$, with a small shift. \\

To better understand why this shift happens in the first transition, we plot the $|\Omega|^2$ for different slices. For this $|\Omega|^2$ is plotted in \Cref{fig:Omega_highres_3block} for slice number 5,200 and 400 in a total number of 400 slices, as in \cref{fig:lowexc_cohinc}. Here on the very left plot in the non propagating limit, similar to the plots in \Cref{section_LER}, Bessel dynamics do not emerge, as there are no multiple scattering dynamics. This can be explained like this: \\

Due to the pulse area theorem in \Cref{pulse_area_theorem_rhoee}, a $\pi$ pulse by $\rho_{ee}$ corresponds to a $2\pi$ pulse by $\rho_{eg}$ (\cref{pulse_area_theorem_rhoge}). Thus no dipole moments are induced, as $\rho_{eg}$ is proportional to dipole moments. That is  why all incident light gets scattered incoherently by $4\pi$ scattering. As we are plotting the coherently scattered light $|\Omega|^2$ in \Cref{fig:Omega_highres_3block}, we see these dips by the multiples of $\pi$ .\\

    \caption{$|\Omega|^2$ as a function of excitation and time, three slices 5,200 and 400 are plotted to observe the excitation shift by $\mathcal{A} = \pi$. The avoided crossing gets shifted to minimally higher excitations by increasing target thickness. Observing the propagation effects in the both right plots in \Cref{fig:Omega_highres_3block}, we can observe the shift of this transition in excitation. With increasing thickness of the target, the transition also gets shifted to higher excitations. This can be explained with increasing thickness of the target, the incident pulse gets more absorbed with propagation, thus one has to have started with a higher pulse area, in order to excite the nuclei to $\pi$ on average. So the transition gets shifted in minimally higher excitations with a thicker target. This effect can be seen more substantially in thicker targets in the next subsection.\\



\subsection{Coherent radiation in thicker targets}
Instead of solving for $z_\mathrm{max} = 0.7$ as given in \Cref{table:the_parameters}, we can also solve for thicker targets, which is experimentally more feasible. In \Cref{fig:Omega_6block}, Maxwell-Bloch equations were solved for 6 different thicknesses ranging from $0.25\;\mu m$
to $4\;\mu m$. Thicker targets can also be calculated but to avoid numerical instabilities, we have to calculate with a much bigger number of slices $N$, which results in a very long computation time. That is why we sticked to this range of thicknesses.\\

In \Cref{fig:Omega_6block} the same structures can be seen as in \cref{fig:Omega_highexc}: the time shift of the Bessel beats until the population inversion and the connection of the Bessel beats to the next beats around inversion. The reason why more Bessel beats emerge is from the simple cause that with different $z_\mathrm{max}$ we get a different b, defined in \cref{betazmax}, which determines the dips of the Bessel functions.\\

The shift of the crossings can also be seen in \Cref{fig:Omega_6block}. The crossing is around $\mathcal{A} = 1 \pi$ for $z_\mathrm{max} = 0.25$, which then increases to as high as $\mathcal{A} = 1.13\pi$ for $z_\mathrm{max} = 4$ , which does support our discussion in \Cref{section_omegamicroscope}.\\

As a next step, we analyze the dynamics of $\rho_{eg}$ and $\rho_{ee}$ as a function of time, which can be seen in \Cref{fig:highres_rhoegrhoee}. Here $\rho_{eg}$ shows the same dynamics as in \cref{fig:lowexc_omegarhoeg}, which results from the Bessel function of order 0. But looking at \cref{fig:highres_rhoegrhoee}, we can see the same shifts of the Bessel beats as in \cref{fig:Omega_highexc} in time as the excitation increases. Around population inversion, the beats connect to the next Bessel beats, also similar to $|\Omega|^2$ in \Cref{fig:Omega_highexc}. This behaviour is expected as $\Omega$ and $\rho_{eg}$ are strongly related to each other.\\

On the right side of the \Cref{fig:highres_rhoegrhoee}, $\rho_{ee} $ is plotted as a function of time and excitation. An interesting feature in $\rho_{ee}$ is that the structures, which can be seen in low excitation regime vanishes at higher excitations. This can be explained in a similar way as we discussed in \Cref{section:propeffectsLER}: The \Cref{rhoee} consists of two parts, the first part is the spontaneous decay and the second part is the coupling to $\rho_{eg}$. In LER, $\rho_{ee}$ is small due to the pulse area theorem in  \Cref{pulse_area_theorem_rhoee}. As a result the exponential decay is not so strong and we see the structures from $\rho_{eg}$ also in the $\rho_{ee}$. Going beyond the low excitation regime, $\rho_{ee}(t)$ increases significantly and spontaneous decay becomes the dominant part in \Cref{rhoee}. That is why the structures in $\rho_{ee}$ washes out with increasing excitations in \Cref{fig:highres_rhoegrhoee}.\\

Around population inversion $\mathcal{A} = \pi $ in \Cref{fig:highres_rhoegrhoee}, we can see the phenomena that we discussed related to  \Cref{fig:Omega_highres_3block}. There is no dipole induced by $\mathcal{A} = \pi$, as $\rho_{eg}$ is zero. This can also be seen in \Cref{fig:highres_rhoegrhoee}, where around population inversion induced dipole moments are low and most of the incoming light would be transferred to incoherent light, which can be seen in $\rho_{ee}$, on the right side of \cref{fig:highres_rhoegrhoee}.

\subsection{Time evolution of $\rho_{ee}$ in target}

In the low excitation regime, we can see the structures that result due to the coupling of $\rho_{ee}$ and $\rho_{eg}$. These structures do get washed out by $\mathcal{A} = 0.5\pi$ and can no longer be seen in the inversion limit. The mechanism of these effects was discussed in \Cref{section:rhoegrhoeebeyondler}.

\subsection{Incoherent radiation in higher excitation}
As a next step we can calculate the total incoherent radiation, which is the sum of all $\rho_{ee}$ emitted from all the slices in the target, similar to \Cref{fig:summed_rhoee}. For this we plot the summed $\rho_{ee}$ for the same excitation regime as in the previous plots on the left side in \Cref{fig:summedrhoee_beyondler}. On the left side, we plot 5 different solutions from the 2D plot, to gain a better understanding of the shape of the curves. \\

In \Cref{fig:summed_rhoee} the highest emitted incoherent radiation can be seen at  $\mathcal{A} = \pi$. This is due to the fact that by population inversion all the atoms get excited without inducing dipole moments, which results in a incoherent radiation. These excited atoms then start to decay, which is coupled to $\rho_{eg}$ and then to $\Omega$, so we also see some coherent radiation after some initial time. \\

Another interesting feature of the incoherent light is, in the relatively low excitation regime, the slight oscillations can be seen in \Cref{fig:summedrhoee_beyondler}, which was discussed in \Cref{section:propeffectsLER} and can be explained as the interference of different Bessel beats corresponding to the different thicknesses in the target. At last, we can analyse the curve by $2\pi$ pulse. Here the atoms are close to the ground state after the incident light pulse. So there is hardly any coherent and incoherent radiation, which can also be seen in the black curve in \Cref{section:rhoegrhoeebeyondler}.\\


\subsection{Time shifts as a signature of non linear excitation}

From \Cref{fig:Omega_6block}, we can see that the effect of time shift is dependent on the target thickness. Here we propose a signature of time shift dependent on the excitation for different target thicknesses. For this we calculated the position of the first dips in \Cref{fig:Omega_6block} and plotted the relative time shift of the first Bessel beats as a function of excitation and for different target thicknesses, which can be seen in \Cref{fig:relative_timeshift}.\\

In \Cref{fig:relative_timeshift} the relative time shifts are plotted ranging from $0.5$ to $5 \,z_\mathrm{max}$, which equals to same physical thickness in $\mu m $, as $1\, z_\mathrm{max} \approx 1\, \mu m $, due to \cref{b_conversion} and \cref{betazmax}. A conservative guess for time resolution would be $1\,ns$ which equals to $t = 0.0035 \gamma^{-1}$ where in present experiments $0.1 \,ns$ and better time resolutions can be achieved. Also by using measuring schemes and more focussing, going to higher excitations are now within reach \cite{dominik_higherexcitation}. So it is not unrealistic that this effect can actually be measured in an NFS setup.

\subsection{Comparison with Burnham-Chiao in the non-decaying limit}



\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width = 1\textwidth]{data/highlow_exc/beyond_ler/burnhamchiao_2.png}
    \caption{$|\Omega|^2$ as a function of time and excitation, solved with two different methods. On the left side as a solution of ODE defined in \Cref{burnham_2}, on the right side solved with Maxwell-Bloch equations where we used the basis set of values from \Cref{table:the_parameters} and set the $\gamma =0$ to compare the results with \cite{BURNHAM1969}. Time scale on the right side is scaled for comparison with the left side, which is given in $\gamma $, with $\gamma = 141\, ns$ for $\alpha$-Iron.}
    \label{fig:comparisonburnham}
\end{figure}


Until now there is not an explanation given for the time shift and the transition. To understand this phenomena better, we are going to compare our results with the results from Burnham-Chiao \cite{BURNHAM1969}. They calculated the coherently scattered light $\Omega$ and the averaged dipole moments, which is proportional to $\rho_{eg}$ in a setting, where they could express the excitation as an initial condition for a Bessel differential equation in \Cref{burnham_2}. More about the theory can be read in \Cref{section:burnhamchiao}.\\

The crucial point is that they took into account times much smaller than the characteristic decay times. To obtain the same results we can set $\gamma =0$, as in a non-decaying limit. The comparison between our model and the model they developed in \cite{BURNHAM1969} can be seen in \Cref{fig:comparisonburnham}. Here on the left side $|\Omega|^2$ plotted by solving the differential equation given in \cref{burnham_diff} by \cite{BURNHAM1969}. The time is given in $\tau_{R}$, which also depends on a couple of parameters dependent on $q$ and $Q$, given in \cref{burnham_diff} and \cref{burnham_1}. In \Cref{fig:comparisonburnham} we scaled the time scale to fit the first Bessel beats with our model.\\

Here the most important point is that we see the same structures as in our model, meaning the time shifts by Bessel beats with increasing excitations. In \cite{BURNHAM1969}, they only defined this model for excitations $0 \le \Theta_0 \le \pi $, where $\Theta_0$ the pulse area is. In \Cref{fig:comparisonburnham} we plotted for excitations out of the definition range, which actually also predicts the dynamics beyond the population inversion.\\

An interesting feature can be seen when comparing the two plots in \Cref{fig:comparisonburnham}. The second Bessel beat comes in a later time span in the plot on the left , which was calculated by using the methods in \cite{BURNHAM1969}, compared to the plot on the right which was calculated with our model. These deviations in the Burnham-Chiao model could have arisen from the approximations they made when calculating the model for higher excitations. On the other hand, no approximations were made with our model.\\

Regarding the non-decaying limit, we can draw conclusions, that the transition around inversions arise purely as a result of the spontaneous emission. Without spontaneous emission, we can only see the time shifts dependent from the excitation but not the transitions around population inversions, where the Bessel beats perfectly connect to the next beats.  \\

Summarizing our findings in \Cref{section:beyondLER}, we analysed the nuclear dynamics and light propagation beyond the low excitation regime, namely at pulse areas spanning from $0\pi$ to $2.5\pi$, which covers the LER, beyond LER, population inversion and beyond the population inversion. Here we discovered interesting structures at around population inversion by $\mathcal{A} = \pi$.\\

Going beyond the LER to inversion, the Bessel beats get shifted in time, which we then proposed this as a signature to determine the non-linear excitations. At population inversion, we see that the Bessel beats perfectly connect to the next Bessel beats. To understand this phenomena better, we went in to a more in depth analyse about how $\rho_{eg}$, $\rho_{ee}$ and the summed incoherent radiation $I_\mathrm{inc}$ evolves in time, in thin targets as well as thick targets. \\

We then solved the differential equation numerically proposed by \cite{BURNHAM1969} to approximate the light propagation in higher excitations beyond LER until population inversion in the non-decaying limit. We recovered the same result in our model in non-decaying limit ($\gamma =0$) as in \cite{BURNHAM1969}. As a result, we could conclude, that the exponential decay plays the main role in creation of the transitions that we see around population inversions.
